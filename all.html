So, you want to be a suzuki parent

Is your child ready?

  • Are they at least three years old and potty trained?
  • Are they able to focus for at least 5 minutes?
  • Are they not already too busy?

Are you ready?

  • Do you have at least 10 minutes a day to focus on violin practice?
  • Can you afford the materials needed as well as the lessons?
  • Do you have time on weekends for occasional recitals and group lessons?
  • Can you do it all with an upbeat, or at least no nonsense, attitude?

What is the Suzuki Method?

Dr. Suzuki preferred to call his violin teaching method talent education. He made the startling discovery that virtually all children learn to speak their native language, no matter how difficult. This led him to design a violin teaching method that mimicked the language learning process.

Ideas behind this method are:

  • Listening and watching others play
  • Delaying note reading until tone production is established
  • Parental assistance at lessons and at home
  • Repetition, repetition, repetition
  • Loving encouragement

I think this method is especially good for learning instruments like the violin, on which tone production is difficult. This method also works better for the very young than traditional violin methods.

For more information on the Suzuki Violin Method, go to the Suzuki Association of the Americas website.

Environment

If you want your child to succeed at violin, you should set up an environment which includes

  • listening to music
  • watching others play
  • practicing regularly

Most people need to practice at least five days per week to make progress in any violin method. Practice should not be long, especially for a beginner, but even a more advanced student shouldnâ€™t be made to practice beyond their endurance level. My high school senior practiced 20-30 minutes a day, and he played very well. For the very young student, much of practice might be watching the parent learn to play. Routine can help violin practice become something one does, like eating dinner or story time.

Listening will make that time pay off more. You can start listening right away. Check out some music CDs at the library and listen to them every day. Bedtime is good, so is indoor play time or homework time. My daughter listened whenever she uses the computer for work or play.

Practice Practice Practice

At some point, most parents find it difficult to get their child to practice.

  • Keep practice time short
  • Start with something easy, end with something fun
  • If something is too frustrating, cut your losses

Materials

You will need a Suzuki Violin method volume 1, violin part, book and CD. Things like tuners and metronomes are useful to have as well, but are not necessary at the very beginning. You will also need a violin.

Most teachers prefer that you see them before you choose an instrument. Many violin sellers size violins too big. Nielsen Violin shop, 342-5880 downtown Omaha, has regular hours and is not too expensive.

  • Nielsen Violin Shop
  • Amesquita Violins
  • The Becker Violin Shop (in Des Moines)

in Des Moines is good if you want to purchase, but I don't think the quality is better if you are renting. I have had better experiences with these than with the local general music shops. They are faster at repairs and usually have better quality instruments, but you can get an adequate violin from most of the music shops. Buying a "violin shaped object" from the internet is quite risky. Buying a used violin from someone who has taken lessons can be a good deal. You can also check out sharmusic.com and ymonline.com and doubtless there are others.
